NOTES ON RHETORIC

 

WHAT IS RHETORIC?

Rhetoric is the study and the practice of persuasion. Ancient theorists (such as Aristotle, Isocrates, and Quintilian) pointed out that people are unique among animals in our ability to form large, complicated societies. Left alone, we would fail. People need rhetoric to cooperate without force. Kenneth Burke defined rhetoric as "the use of language as a symbolic means of inducing cooperation in beings that by nature respond to symbols" (A Rhetoric of Motives [Berkeley: University of California Press, 1969], p. 43).

 

WHAT IS RHETORICAL ANALYSIS?

Rhetorical analysis studies persuasion to judge effectiveness. We analyze so we can judge better and advocate better. First, we suspend agreement. Then we assess artfulness, quality, decorum, and effects. Analysis prepares us to argue more responsibly and effectively.

 

THE RHETORICAL PERSPECTIVE?

A rhetorical perspective shows uncertainty in our beliefs. It warns against absolute conviction and urges us to see merit in opposing cases. It drives us to refine persuasion methods. Rhetorical analysts become cautious believers, considerate listeners, and careful actors.

  • Develop strategies for creating and editing texts
  • Write with audience and purpose in mind

  • Integrate sources accurately
  • Document sources properly
  • Maintain correct grammar and mechanics
