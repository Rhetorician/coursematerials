Cover Letter: Design and Execution

Beginning caveat

Research has consistently shown that at least 1/3 but probably closer to ½ of recruiters and hiring managers do not read cover letters, preferring to go right to the resume. Does that mean that you should not write one? Absolutely not. Employment cover letters help you as a candidate to frame your skills, experiences and goals.

Employment Cover Letter Introduction

There are generally two cover letter families in professional and business writing: Simple “transmittal-based letters” that may cover a report or proposal, and summarize the document’s contents. Employment based cover letters. The most common kinds of cover letters are those associated with resumes or other job-search documents. A cover letter accompanying a report or proposal for example may just be a summary of that document. But an employment cover letter is more as it serves as part of a marketing package—like the resume cover letters must be persuasive as well. Their purpose is not to summarize what lies beneath them but among other things to persuade a reader to turn to the resume.

Rhetoric and Employment Cover Letters

Audience = The person who makes hiring decisions But how do you identify that person? → Informational Interview Research Identify the contact suggest a brief meeting use your SDSU status buy a thank you card and prepare a highlighted version of your resume. You’d be surprised how many prospective employers will agree to an informal meeting or interview particularly from students.

Consider Taking the time to create such an opportunity and ask questions like: “What was your career path? How did you get involved in this field?” “What is the biggest issue this industry faces at the moment?” “How is your company reacting to this issue?” “What do you look for in a potential candidate?” “I’m near graduation. What would you do in my position?’ “I’m interested in getting more information. Can you suggest someone else I can talk to and is it OK to keep you updated on my progress?”

Purpose of the Cover Letter

Generally the purpose of all cover letters is to introduce the subject as it is manifested in the document it covers. Employment cover letters specifically generate interest in your skills education and experience And serve as a bridge between job posting and the resume It can be tempting just to generate interest by content. Afterall perhaps:

  • Have done your work reviewed the samples
  • Have great experience you studied abroad you had great internships etc.
  • Have more information that can fit on your resume.

But good cover letter writers use effective visual design as well. They understand that how a cover letter looks is almost as important as what it says. You need to tell the reader’s eyes where to go. For example before we review the Purdy letter notice the way the letter looks. An important component of professional writing is visual and information design: Chunked information bullet pointed clear simple language Several short paragraphs between 4 and 5 lines that are interrupted by two short bulleted passages But cover letters do more than introduce your resume. In addition to the functions listed on the screen, employment cover letters are important because they:

  • Explain your experiences in a story-like format that works with the information provided in your resume
  • Show the employer that you are individualizing (tailoring) this job application
  • Provide a sample of your written communication skills

It is the first document an employer sees, so it is often the first impression you will make.

Exigency/Genre: Part of an integrated, focused campaign

One overriding fact about the context of employment cover letters—and you need to remember this, perhaps more than any other professional document—readers look to disqualify cover letters and resumes, especially in competitive job climates (like now).

Employment Cover Letter Readers

Because they are read to disqualify, audience is important for employment document because employment documents must be able to convince the reader very quickly that what you are saying is true in order to be effective. So you must have done your homework about the company because only then are you prepared to directly communicate with the organization. That means you need to know as much as you can about how people read resumes so here are some general characteristics of your audience:

  • Read job letters in stacks.
  • Are tired.
  • Often do reading late in the day, less patient with flowery diction and hard to read type or other poor design elements.
  • Are impatient.
  • Will not dig for information. They expect it to jump right out at them.
  • Suddenly become very picky proofreaders.

Summary: Employment Cover Letters

Consider the cover letter as part of a marketing package. Do your homework about the company, about discipline-specific communication types, and as much as you can about the reader. Consider yourself vital and let this come through with humility.
