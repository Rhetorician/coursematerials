Many students have only a vague notion of what is required for them to obtain a job in their intended career field, and even more think they will land a position in an organization without relevant industry experience. This is naïve. If you don't prepare yourself for work in your desired career until after you graduate, you'll be too late. The labor market is very competitive. There are not nearly enough jobs for the number of people graduating each year with bachelor's degrees. Indeed, numerous surveys demonstrate that anywhere between 48-53% of new college and university graduates are unemployed or underemployed in jobs that do not require a bachelor's degree a year after graduation. Furthermore, the average amount of student loan debt is over $40,000.

This project is intended to help you discover what you need to do to prepare yourself for the labor market--before you graduate. I hope you will find it informative and useful to you as you move closer to graduation.

This article illustrates more visual examples that do a good job supplementing their argument. Remember, the goal as a writer is to enable the reader to visualize the data and a good, well placed image can go a long way rhetorically.

    1. Title Page
      • Clearly state the report's title, your full name, and the date of submission.
    2. Nature of the Work
      • Description the profession; include principal responsibilities and typical work environments.
      • Illustrate with examples of the skills/knowledge required for this (and later, the related occupations).
    3. Training, Qualifications, and Advancement
      • Present the education and training necessary for entry into this career, including degrees, certifications, and requisite skills.
      • Discuss process of career advancement: how one progresses to higher roles and the experiences or achievements that facilitate this growth.
    4. Job Outlook
      • Present current trends and future projections for this career.
      • Analyze how changes in the industry, technology advancements, and shifts in regulatory landscapes impact employment opportunities. Provide data or evidence to support your analysis.
    5. Earnings and Wage Projections
      • Explore the range of earnings within this career, from entry-level to experienced positions. Discuss factors that influence wage levels, like industry demand, job complexity, geographic location, and educational background. Be sure to provide a comparative analysis of salary expectations across different sectors within the field.
    6. Alternative Occupations
      • Compare this career with at least three related occupations. Discuss the similarities and differences in job duties, required qualifications, and earnings potential. Assess the feasibility and implications of transitioning between these careers, and be sure to consider factors like job satisfaction and career trajectory.
    7. Impact of Career on Society [Optional]
      • Somewhere in your conclusion, reflect on the significance of this career in broader societal, economic, or other contexts. To this end, you might also consider how pros in this field contribute to economic growth or even just social productivity more generally.
    8. Effective Visuals
      • Integrate visuals (charts, graphs, or tables) to illuminate your analysis. Each visual should be introduced and explained, illustrate and simplify complex data, trends, or relationships. That is, your visuals should provide clarity into trends, comparisons, or projections relevant to your report.
        NOTE: do not use BLS images or you will receive a zero for this part of the assignment.
    9. Conclusion
      • Conclude by personally reflecting on your findings.
      • Assess how this career is consistent with your strengths, weaknesses, and professional aspirations.
      • Outline a substantive plan for personal and professional development (see Growth Plan)

  • MECHANICS
    • Missing or weak transitional words and phrases. I recommend everyone become familiar with these sources and use them in all of your writing for the rest of your life:
      • https://writing.wisc.edu/handbook/style/connectingideas/
      • https://writing.wisc.edu/handbook/style/transitions/
    • Format: Errors in whitespace, length, line-spacing, and alignment
    • Format: In professional writing, block format is used, without indenting, single-spaced, and left-aligned text. Each paragraph should be writing by a line space.
    • In professional discourse it is unconventional to indent paragraphs. This actually disrupts the reading process and is inappropriate outside of academic essays.
    • Formatting and Aesthetics: There should be consistency in terms of proper heading styles, fonts, margins, and white space. This should be a polished, professional document.
    • Chunking: describes the process splitting paragraphs into small, digestible pieces or "chunks" of information to make reading easier.
    • Professionalism/objectivity: In profession writing, don’t use inclusive language—e.g., "we," "us," "our," or second person "you" statements.
    • Diction and sentence structure
    • Wordiness
    • Passive voice:
    • Insincere Tone—usually connected to wordiness and inflated diction
    • Recall that you should insert a parenthetical and subsequently reference the "BLS." Applied, it would look something like: “…the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), …”
    • Always ground your claims in authoritative sources. Citations should be used to validate technical claims.
  • CONTENT DEVELOPMENT AND ANALYSIS
    • Examples of skills/knowledge required for this and related occupations. Rather than simply stating they require analytical, communication, interpersonal, etc., skills, you needed to demonstrate how these skills are connected to the field.
    • Job outlook: should consider factors that influence growth or decline. This means accounting for things like industry trends, technology, or even regulations, all of which would lend more credibility to your analysis.
      • No analysis of the factors that influence earning and wage projections for the job in different industries.
      • No consideration of trends and/or driving factors that affect earning and wage projections
    • Weak discussion of potential for advancement: Should discuss typical career paths along with skills or experiences that can lead to more opportunities for growth.
    • Weak earnings and wage projections: should include range of salaries across different experience levels or geographic locations.
    • Weak comparative analysis: should consider not only the similarities/differences in required qualifications but also the associated career trajectories and job satisfaction associated with each role.
  • IMAGES
    • Bad/inadequate images.
      • A visual should clarify trends, relationships, establish correlation, or visualize things that either difficult to conceptualize or would be otherwise tedious to read.
    • Always introduce, explain, or interpret a visual, and place it for maximum effect.
      • Use visuals with—not as a substitute for—written work.
      • Refer to a visual before it appears, on the same page if possible.
      • State what you want the readers to learn from it.
    • Data Presentation and Analysis: must interpret and discuss the figures, graphs, and tables.
  • CONCLUSION
    • Weak growth plan: this is by far the #1 weakness I see in reports. In the last two years they have only gotten worse with the rise of AI. Write it yourself and follow the posted guidance.
